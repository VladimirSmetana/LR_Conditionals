Topic 1 Conditionals in English

What are conditionals in English grammar? Sometimes we call
them 'if clauses'. They describe the result of something that
might happen (in the present or future) or might have happened
but didn't (in the past) . They are made using different
English verb tenses.

Information

Zero Conditional
Usage: For facts, general truths, and scientific facts. It describes situations that are always true
Structure: If/When + Present Simple, ... Present Simple.
If you heat ice, it melts.
When the sun sets, it gets dark.
If people don't drink water, they become dehydrated.
First Conditional
Usage: For real and possible situations in the future. It describes a likely outcome of a specific condition.
Structure: If + Present Simple, ... will + base verb.
If it rains tomorrow, we will cancel the picnic.
If you study hard, you will pass the exam.
If I have time, I might go to the gym.
Second Conditional
Usage: For unreal, imaginary, or hypothetical situations in the present or future. It describes unlikely or impossible outcomes. It is also used to give advice.
Structure: If + Past Simple, ... would/could/might + base verb.
If I won the lottery, I would travel the world. (I probably won't win)
If I were you, I would apologize. (Giving advice - I am not you)
If he had a car, he could drive us to the party. (He doesn't have a car)
Third Conditional
Usage: For hypothetical situations in the past. It describes a situation that did NOT happen and its imaginary consequence. It's often used to express regret or criticism.
Structure: If + Past Perfect, ... would/could/might + have + Past Participle (V3).
If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam. (But I didn't study hard, and I failed.)
She would have been late if her flight had been delayed. (But her flight wasn't delayed, so she wasn't late.)
If they had taken the map, they couldn't have gotten lost. (But they didn't take it, so they got lost.)
